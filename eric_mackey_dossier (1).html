§ II

The Ikigai

A Japanese concept: the intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. The center, for you, is not a job. It is a posture.

What you love
What you’re good at
What the world needs

Entrepreneurship
What you love
Making things from nothing. The cold-start problem. Naming a thing into existence and watching strangers reach for it.
What you’re good at
Pattern recognition across systems — finance, brand, product, people. Translating taste into go-to-market. Frameworks that survive contact with reality.
What the world needs
Companies built on honest value, not extracted attention. Products that respect the user. Better defaults.
What you can be paid for
Founder. Operator. Investor. Capital allocator with conviction.
"You don't become an entrepreneur. You stop pretending you're anything else."

§ V

The Pantheon

Eight people you study. Not to imitate, but to triangulate. The patterns across them are clues to who you're becoming.

i

Steve Jobs

Taste as a weapon. Saying no a thousand times so the one yes can be perfect.

ii

Phil Knight

The Oregon way. Long-game obsession dressed as nonchalance. Run your race.

iii

Rick Rubin

The listener's ear. Strip until what remains is true. Stillness as production technique.

iv

Anthony Bourdain

Curiosity as religion. Show up hungry, leave changed. Honor the strangers who feed you.

v

Paul Graham

The essay as instrument of thought. Founder mode. Make something people want — and write it down.

vi

Kanye West

Refusal to be small. Aesthetic conviction as moral position. The cost of swinging is missing.

vii

Donald Glover

Multi-hyphenate fluency. Comedy, music, prestige drama, all the same hand. Refuse the lane.

viii

Norman Foster

Form follows imagination. Engineering as poetry. The discipline that makes lightness possible.

§ VII

The Practices

Identity is downstream of habit. Three live commitments — small enough to do, durable enough to compound.

● Live

NYT Top 100 of the 21st Century

Working through the canon, one screening at a time. Letterboxd as ledger.

● Live

Take the Chair

One leadership role this academic year you didn't already have — fund president, club chair, conference organizer. The room sees you differently when you sit at the head of it.

● Live

The Cold Outreach Rule

One thoughtful email a week to someone two steps above you. Most won't respond. Two will. One of those will change your trajectory.
